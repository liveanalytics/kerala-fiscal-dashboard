Deficit & Debt Trend
Every year Kerala spends more than it earns — this chart shows how big that gap is, as a share of the State's total economy. The dotted line is the legal limit Kerala promised to stay under. Every time the red line crosses above it, that promise was broken.
Executive SummaryRealised deficits have stayed above the State's FRBM targets across the decade, with borrowing rising to fund the gap.
Early Warning Signals
Treasury Balance vs Borrowing
The bars show how much cash was left in Kerala's treasury at the end of each year. The orange line shows how much new money was borrowed that same year. When borrowing keeps rising but the cash balance stays flat, it means every rupee borrowed was spent before the year ended — nothing was saved.
Own Tax Revenue: Budgeted vs Realised
At the start of each year Kerala sets a tax collection target (light bars). The dark bars show what was actually collected. The orange line tracks how close the State got — 100% means it hit the target exactly. Every year below 100% means a shortfall that had to be covered by borrowing.

Revenue & Fiscal Deficit vs Target
Think of two kinds of loss: the 'day-to-day loss' (revenue deficit) is the gap just from routine running costs like salaries and pensions. The 'total loss' (fiscal deficit) adds in all other spending too. Both have legal limits shown as dotted lines — crossing above means Kerala spent beyond what the law allows.
Debt vs FRBM Ceiling
This shows Kerala's total debt pile compared to the size of its economy. The dotted red line is the legal ceiling Kerala agreed to — if the debt line rises above it, Kerala has borrowed more than it legally should. As you can see, it has stayed above the limit for most of the decade.
Own Tax Revenue
Light bars are Kerala's own tax targets; dark bars are what was actually collected. Every gap between them is money Kerala expected but never received — and had to borrow instead to keep the government running.
Expenditure: Budget vs Actuals
Kerala consistently spends more on day-to-day running costs (salaries, pensions) than planned, while spending less than planned on building new infrastructure and assets. In plain terms: more money going out on routine bills, less money going into the future.
Central Transfers 2026-27
Every year Kerala assumes it will receive a certain amount of money from the Central Government. The lighter bar is the assumption; the darker bar is what was actually received. The gap is money Kerala counted on but never got — forcing it to borrow to fill the hole.

Treasury Balance vs Open Market Borrowing
The bars show how much cash Kerala had left at year end. The orange line shows how much Kerala borrowed by selling bonds that year. When the orange line climbs steeply while the bars stay flat, it means Kerala is borrowing more and more — but spending every rupee immediately, keeping nothing in reserve.
Cash StressA positive year-end balance often reflects a late surge in March borrowing rather than underlying strength.
Liquidity Alerts
Days in WMA & Overdraft (2011–2026)
On some days Kerala's treasury runs completely empty. On those days the State borrows emergency funds from the Reserve Bank of India just to pay salaries and bills. Yellow bars show days of emergency borrowing (called WMA); red bars show days when even that limit was crossed and Kerala went into overdraft. In 2025, this happened on 262 out of 365 days.
Inherited Arrears by Category
These are bills Kerala has not yet paid — salary allowances owed to government staff, pension relief owed to retirees, payments owed to contractors, and welfare scheme dues. Each bar shows one category. The new government inherited all of this as unpaid debt on day one — totalling ₹48,733 crore.
Quick Reference
WMA (Ways & Means Advance) = Short-term emergency loan from RBI when treasury runs empty Overdraft (OD) = Borrowing beyond even the emergency WMA limit — the last resort OMB (Open Market Borrowing) = Bonds Kerala sells to banks and investors to raise money Arrears = Bills that are due and overdue but not yet paid Treasury = The government's bank account where all income and spending flows

Accumulated Losses of All PSEs
Kerala owns dozens of companies — buses (KSRTC), electricity (KSEB), water, construction and more. This chart shows the total losses these companies have accumulated over the years that were never recovered. It is not last year's loss alone — it is the entire mountain of losses built up over time — growing from ₹31,571 crore in 2021-22 to ₹78,851 crore in 2024-25.
Drain on BudgetCombined losses grew from ₹31,571 cr (2021-22) to ₹78,851 cr (2024-25) in just four years, while the State keeps funding these enterprises.
Support Given vs Returns Received
The tall bars show money Kerala puts into its State-owned companies every year — subsidies, loans and budget support. The short bars show what comes back as profit or dividend. The huge gap between them is taxpayer money that goes in and never comes back.
KSEBL: Revenue, Expenditure & Profit
Blue bars = total income of Kerala's electricity company (KSEB). Red bars = total costs. The line shows the difference — above zero is profit, below zero is loss. When the line dips below the centre, KSEB spent more than it earned that year.
KSRTC vs Water Authority: Net Worth
Net worth = what a company owns minus what it owes. When net worth falls below zero (below the centre line) it means the company's debts are bigger than everything it owns — technically bankrupt. Kerala's bus corporation (KSRTC) has been in this condition every year shown here.
Quick Reference
PSE / SLPE = State-owned company (e.g. KSRTC buses, KSEB electricity, KWA water) Accumulated loss = Total of all past losses never recovered — not just this year's loss Net worth = What a company owns minus what it owes — negative means technically bankrupt Budgetary support = Taxpayer money given to state companies to keep them running KIIFB = Kerala Infrastructure Investment Fund — borrows separately outside the main budget

Plan Outlay vs Expenditure
Light bars = the development budget officially approved at the start of the year. Dark bars = what was actually spent. When dark exceeds light, Kerala spent more than it planned — often because Central Government scheme costs and old pending bills had to be cleared.
Composition by Service Category
Of every ₹100 Kerala spends on development, this chart shows how much goes to health and education, how much to roads and infrastructure, how much to government administration, and how much just to repaying old loans. Watch how the slices change year by year.
Plan Share of Total Spending
This line shows what share of Kerala's total spending actually goes toward new development — building things, running schemes. A falling line means salaries, pensions and interest payments are eating up more and more of the budget, leaving less and less for actual development work.
Shrinking SpaceAs salaries, pensions and interest claim more of the budget, the slice left for development has fallen to its lowest in the decade.
SCSP & TSP Delivery (% of Outlay)
By law, a fixed portion of Kerala's development budget must be spent on welfare of Scheduled Caste (SC) and Scheduled Tribe (ST) communities. This chart shows how much of that reserved money was actually spent. 100% means fully delivered; below 100% means welfare money that was reserved but went unspent.
Quick Reference
Plan / Development spending = Money for new projects — roads, buildings, schemes SCSP = Scheduled Caste Sub-Plan — budget ring-fenced by law for SC community welfare TSP = Tribal Sub-Plan — budget ring-fenced by law for ST community welfare Outlay = The approved development budget at the start of the year CSS = Centrally Sponsored Schemes — projects funded jointly by Centre and State
